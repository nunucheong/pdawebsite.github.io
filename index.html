PART 1:
Understanding Your Prostate And It's Functions

What is Prostate?

The prostate is part of the male reproductive system. It is below the bladder and in front of the rectum in the pelvic region. The prostate is about the size of a chestnut when it is healthy. It surrounds the tube called the urethra that carries urine and semen out of the penis.

Prostate is a gland that helps make semen which is released during ejaculation. The prostate produces another substance called PSA (prostate specific antigen). PSA level can be higher than normal in men with prostate cancer as well as in some other benign prostate conditions such as BPH/benign hyperplasia (prostate enlargement) or prostatitis (infection of the prostate).

What Can Happen To The Prostate

Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia or BPH

As your get older, the prostate can grow. If the prostate becomes too large, it can press on the urethra (tube that carries urine and semen out of the penis). This may cause difficulty in urination.

Prostate Cancer

The prostate can also develop cancer. The cancer is localized when it is in the early stage (Stage 1 and 2). It can spread to the nearby tissue such as the seminal vesicles, or later get into the bloodstream and lymphatic system and spread to other parts of the body such as bones and lymph nodes (stage 3 and 4).

Prostatitis

The prostate becomes inflamed. It happens when there is an infection in the are of prostate.

PART 2:
Overview of Prostate Cancer

How Is Prostate Cancer Different From Other Cancer?

Prostate cancer is a very different kind of cancer. Most prostate cancers grow very slowly and do not cause problems especially for those with early stage prostate cancer. Thus, you can take your time to decide on your treatment choices. There is no hurry to start the treatment immediately.

Most men with prostate cancer do not die from prostate cancer. Because it is slow growing, men often die from other conditions such as heart attack. However, a few prostate cancers grow more quickly and there is a chance it could grow and spread and even cause death.

Medical Tests

Your doctor will take into account of your general health, the result of your tests and examinations, and the Gleason score (a method to grade prostate cancer) of your cancer when talking with you about your treatment choices.

The common tests are:

Grading Of The Cancer (Gleason Score)

This is categorized based on variouexamples taken from prostate biopsy and examined under a microscope. This score tells how different the prostate cancer tissue looks from normal prostate tissue and how likely it is that the cancer will grow or spread.

PSA Test

The PSA is a blood test. The lower the PSA, the better the chances are that treatment will be successful.

Staging Of The Tumour

The tests include digital rectal examination, bone scan, MRI or CT scane. The lower the stage, the better the chance of a successful treatment.

Who Are The Doctors Who Manage Prostate Cancer?

The doctors involve in managing your cancer are mainly the urologists and oncologists.

Urologists are doctors who treat diseases of the urinary system and male sex organs. They are the surgeons who take the biopsy from you and they are usually the first doctors who tell you about your disease. They carry out surgery to remove the cancer.

Oncologists are doctorrs who specialize in treating cancer using chemotherapy, hormone therapy and radiation. They are doctors who are going to manage your radiation therapy if you choose this treatment. You need to see the oncologist for further understanding of the radiation procedures and its side effect. They will arrange follow up with you if you choose radiation as your treatment.

What Are The Treatment Choices For Early Stage Prostate Cancer?

Observation

Your doctor will monitor the progress of your prostate cancer regularly through follow-ups. There is no surgery or radiation treatment given. Side effects of those treatments are avoided.

Active Treatments

Surgery and radiotherapy are two other treatment choices. They may cure you, but they may also cause side effects, such as problems with controlling your bladder and bowel as well as affecting your sexual function (problem with erection).

How Does Your Doctor Classify Your Risk Group

Prostate Cancer Risk Group

Early prostate cancer can be classified based on PSA level, stage and grade (Gleason Score). The risk refers to the chance of the cancer growing or spreading over time.

  • Low-risk: not likely to grow or spread for many years.
  • Intermediate-risk: not likely to grow of spread for a few years
  • High-risk: may grow or spread within a few years.

If you are in low or intermediate-risk group, you can have the options of choosing observation (watchful waiting or active surveillance), surgery or radiotherapy. If you are in the high risk groups, the doctor would recommend that you choose either surgery or radiation because there is a higher risk for progression of the prostate cancer.

Early-stage patient

Active Surveillance

Procedure
  • Involves regular follow up with examinations and tests.
  • PSA and prostate examination will be done every few months and prostate biopsy every 1 to 3 years.
  • Active treatment such as surgery or radiotheraphy still remain as options in case of disease progression.
Suitable for
Patient who have small cancer confined to the prostate gland and it doesnt grow fast.

Watchful Waiting

Procedure
  • Involves less frequent follow up and examinations than active surveillance
  • Only done if there is concern that the cancer is growing too fast or patient develop symptom.
Suitable for
Patient who is old and have a lot of serious health problem.

Advantages

Can avoid complications of active treatment such as :
  • Trouble controlling bladder or bowels
  • Trouble having an erection
Can change your treatment option
  • You can always change your treatment option to either surgery or radiotheraphy.
Cheaper cost
  • Cost is cheaper compared to active treatment (surgery and radiotheraphy).But active surveillance is more costly than watchful waiting as it require more frequent testing and follow up.

Disadvantages

Feel worried or anxious
  • You may feel worried or anxious about living with cancer and putting off the treatment.
Follow up
  • The cancer needs to be followed closely.
Time consuming
  • Patient need to go for blood tests and biopsies more frequently (in case of active surveillance)
Cancer may spread
  • The cancer may spread and become harder to treat. However, the chance is low if the prostate cancer risk of progression is low.

What do I need to know about surgery ?

Suitable For
Early-stage patient in good health
Admission Duration
One or more days
Outcome
Entire prostate gland will be removed, sometimes lymph gland (nodes) will also be removed.
Surgery Duration
Usually takes about 2 to 4 hours

Open Prostatectomy

Procedure
Prostate is removed through a single long cut made in the abdomen from a point below the belly button to just above the pubic bone.
Cost
  • Government Hospital: RM500 - 2,000
  • Private Hospital: RM20,000 - 25,000

Laparoscopic/ Robotic Surgery

Procedure
A laparoscope is used to remove the prostate. This surgery is done through 4 to 6 small cuts in the belly button and the abdomen.
Cost
Please refer to your doctor.

Comparison between Open Prostatectomy and Laparoscopic

Similarities
  • Result of surgery are the same
  • Type of surgery doesn't matter as long as the surgeon is experienced
Differences
  • Laparoscopic may cause less pain, bleeding and require shorter hospital stay

Post-surgery care

Urination Aid
  • A tube(catheter) will be left in the bladder to drain the urine
  • It will be there for 1-2 weeks to help the wound heal
Rest
  • You are advised to rest and avoid strenuous activities for 4-6 weeks before resuming your routine activities
Diet
  • Advised to have a healthy diet
  • Advised to do appropriate physical activities
  • No special food restriction

Advantages

  • Offers a cure if the cancer is removed completely.

Disadvantages (1/2)

Sometimes not all cancer cells are removed.
  • Need additional treatment such as radiation to remove remaining cancer cells.
Complications of the surgery :
  • Bleeding that require blood transfusion.
  • Blood clots in legs or lungs
  • Wound infection
Problems of controlling urine (Incontinence)
  • Urine may leak when cough, sneeze, strain or change positions suddenly
  • May last up to several months
  • Don't need special treatment
  • 92 of 100 men get better on their own if they had leaking of urine after surgery. The remaining does not get better
  • Can use pad to protect clothes
  • Or undergo special surgery to control leakage
  • Younger men have fewer problem controlling their urine
Problem passing urine
  • Passage of urine improves after surgery for most men
  • Sometimes, scars can be form inside urethra (known as urethral stricture)
    • Occurs in 2-9 men of 100
    • Find hard to pass urine
    • Need procedure to unblock narrowing of urethra

Disadvantages (2/2)

Erection Problem Erectile Dysfunction,ED)
  • Problem having an erection
  • Affect feeling about sex and yourself
    • 80 of 100 have long term ED, it depends on extent of the surgery
  • The risk will be lowered if you :
    • Have good erection before surgery
    • Have nerve-sparing surgery
    • Younger
  • Can be treated with :
    • Oral tablets
    • Vacuum devices
    • Injections
    • Penile implant
Risk of Death
  • Very rare (may happen in 1-2 men of 1000!)

What do I need to know about radiotherapy ?

How it works
  • High-energy x-rays are used to destroy cancer cells in the prostate glands.
  • It can be given from either:
    • outside: External beam radiotherapy (EBRT)
    • inside: Brachytherapy
Complementary Hormone Therapy
If you choose to have radiotherapy treatment, hormone therapy might be suggested to achieve better long term outcome or to reduce the prostate size before radiotherapy
Cost
  • Government Hospital: RM500 - 5,000
  • Private Hospital: Up to RM15,000

External Beam Radiotherapy (EBRT)

Aim
  • To improve the control of cancer and lower the chance of long term side effects
Procedure
  • Given from outside the body and is directed specifically at the prostate gland.
  • Daily session from Monday to Friday and will take about 10-20 minutes for each sessions.
Pre-treatment
  • Patient has to undergo a planning session called simulation
    • To ensure the radiation is targeted at the area to be treated.
    • To increase the chance of cure.
    • Spare normal tissues as much as possible to reduce side effects.
    • Simulation my involve one or two visits to the hospital
    • Each session will last about 20-30 minutes
Techniques of EBRT
  • Conformal Radiotherapy (CRT)
  • Intensity Modulated Radiation Treatment (IMRT)
  • Image Guided Radiation Treatment (IGRT)
  • Stereotactic Radiotherapy (SRT)

Brachytherapy

Procedure
  • Brachytherapy is directed specifically at the prostate gland but is given from inside the gland itself.
  • It will destroy cancer cells from within the tumour without causing too much damage to the normal tissues around the prostate.
Not suitable for
  • Patient who have undergo surgery to the prostate gland,urinary symptoms or large prostate size.
Pre-treatment
  • Patient has to undergo an ultrasound to guide the insertion of the radioactive seeds or applicator needles.
    • Done in operting room under general anesthesia.
    • Might be able to go home on the same day.

Advantages

Disease is cured
  • If the radiotherapy killed all cancer cells,patient can be free from the disease for the rest of his life.
Lower probability of Erectile Dysfunction
  • Erectile dysfunction is less likely to happen with radiotherapy compared to surgery,but more likely than with observation.
Fewer urine leaking problems
  • Radiotherapy has fewer problems with urine leaking compared to surgery.
Not admitted in hospital
  • Patient do not need to be admitted to the hospital unlike surgery.

Disadvantages

Radiation may not kill all cancer cells.
  • If radiotherapy does not cure your cancer, surgery may be necessary. However surgery may be more difficult because of scarring around the prostate after radiotherapy.
Regular follow up
  • You will be seen regularly by your oncologists during radiotherapy to monitor for and treat side effects that usually will occur two weeks after starting EBRT.
Temporary Side effects
  • Tiredness
  • Mild skin redness and dryness
  • Urinary symptoms
  • Bowel symptoms
  • Discomfort in abdomen
